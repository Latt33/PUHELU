Model Audit & Adjustments

The following changes were made to the initial draft model after a systematic audit of every assumption. Each adjustment is listed with the original value, the corrected value, and the reasoning.

1. Multi-touch screening (was: single-touch per year)

Original: Each patient had one chance per year to complete the form. 115,000 eligible × 92% × 35% × 70% = ~26,000 screened.

Problem: Patients visit primary care ~3 times per year (THL Avohilmo data: 2.5–3.5 general population; smokers higher; Helsinki 25–30% below national avg). Each visit sends an SMS. The probability of completing at least once compounds across visits.

Fix: Annual screening probability = 1 − (1 − per-SMS rate)visits/yr. With 3 visits and 22% per-SMS completion: ~53% annual probability → ~61,000 screened/yr. Roughly 2.4× the original estimate.

2. Population-level pool depletion (was: screened-subset only)

Original: The undiagnosed pool was set to COPD cases within one year's screened subset (~2,300). This meant the pool depleted too fast because different patients get screened in different years.

Problem: You don't screen the same 26,000 people every year. The actual undiagnosed pool in the full eligible population is ~10,000 (115,000 × 18% × 49%).

Fix: Both the true-positive and false-positive pools are now tracked at the full eligible population level. Each year, a fraction of the remaining pool gets screened (based on annual screening probability), and diagnosed/cleared patients are removed. This produces a more realistic, slower decay in new diagnoses.

3. False-positive spirometry depletion (was: flat every year)

Original: False-positive spirometry costs were fixed every year — same non-COPD patients get flagged, same spirometry gets ordered.

Problem: A doctor who ordered spirometry for a patient last year and got a negative result won't re-order it this year. The EHR would also show the prior negative result.

Fix: A "never-tested non-COPD pool" now tracks patients who haven't yet had negative spirometry. Each year's FP spirometry referrals deplete this pool. FP costs now decline over time as patients get cleared. This is conservative on costs (overestimates later-year spirometry spend less).

4. Mutually exclusive savings categories (was: overlapping)

Original: Three savings streams: hospitalisation avoidance (€450/pt/yr), severity trajectory shift (€9,250 lifetime), and ER avoidance (€59/pt/yr). The severity shift figure included hospitalisation cost differences, creating double-counting estimated at 15–25%.

Fix: Two mutually exclusive categories: Acute care avoidance (hospitalisations + ER = €509/pt/yr) and Disease progression (COPD-specific medication + outpatient costs only, €218/yr delta × 40% progression rate). No hospitalisation component in the progression savings.

5. Treatment adherence discount (was: 100% adherence assumed)

Original: All diagnosed patients were assumed to start treatment and stay on it, receiving the full benefit of early diagnosis.

Problem: Real-world medication adherence: ~80% of prescriptions are picked up from the pharmacy, and ~80% of picked-up doses are actually taken. Effective adherence: 64%.

Fix: All savings streams are multiplied by 0.64. This reduces per-patient savings by 36% — a significant and necessary correction.

6. QALY per diagnosis: 1.0 → 0.5

Original: 1.0 QALY per early diagnosis, based on 5 years earlier detection × 0.2 QALY/year.

Problem: Literature central estimate is ~0.25 QALY/detected case (Lambe 2019, Jordan 2014). 0.5 is at the optimistic end (Lokke 2012, TLV Sweden), defensible only for Stage I detection with maximum lead time.

Fix: Base case now uses 0.5 QALY. This is optimistic but defensible given PUHELU targets early-stage detection (5–7 year lead time). The cost/QALY metric doubled as a result, but remains well within Finnish thresholds (€30,000–50,000/QALY).

7. Medication costs excluded from county P&L

Original audit flagged: Newly diagnosed patients going on COPD medication (€1,000–1,200/yr) represents a real cost that wasn't modelled.

Resolution: In Finland, outpatient prescription medication is reimbursed by Kela (65% under special reimbursement code 203) and paid out-of-pocket by the patient (35%, capped at ~€627/yr). The wellbeing county pays €0 for outpatient medications. This is therefore not a county cost and correctly excluded from the county P&L.

8. Discount rate added (was: nominal values)

Original: All future values in nominal terms with no time-value-of-money adjustment.

Fix: 3.5% annual discount rate applied to all future savings (standard Finnish public-sector rate). This reduces 5-year NPV of savings by ~15–20%.

9. GP flag review cost added (was: not modelled)

Original: No cost for physician time spent reviewing flags and discussing with patients.

Fix: €15 per spirometry referral generated (~5 minutes of loaded GP time at ~€110/hr). Small per-unit but adds up across thousands of referrals. Included for conservatism, even though the system is designed to integrate seamlessly into existing pre-appointment review.

10. Physician engagement adaptation (was: fixed rate)

Original: Physician engagement was a flat percentage every year, ignoring that doctors learn from outcomes.

Fix: Engagement now drifts based on the positive predictive value (PPV) of flags. If PPV > 45% (doctors see flags leading to real diagnoses), engagement increases +3%/yr. If PPV < 25% (too many false alarms), engagement drops −5%/yr. This creates a feedback loop: good screening accuracy sustains physician trust.

11. Pharma revenue: 25% → 12% market share

Original: 25% market share for a single pharma partner, applied to all new COPD patients.

Problem: PUHELU catches GOLD A/B patients. First-line therapy is LAMA monotherapy (Boehringer Spiriva dominates ~55%). A single pharma partner with ICS-focused portfolio has no leading first-line product — they capture value only when ~30–40% of patients escalate to ICS-containing therapy.

Fix: Weighted 5-year average: ~12% for a single partner. Revenue weighted by remaining model years per cohort. The pharma business case is broader than medication revenue: real-world evidence, disease awareness, EU replication model, health system partnerships. Model now uses generic “pharma partner” rather than naming a specific company.

12. Pharma-led funding model removed

Original: Three funding models: Public, Pharma-Led, Partnership.

Problem: A screening tool funded entirely by the company that sells the medications it generates prescriptions for is a credibility problem. No healthcare system would trust that structure.

Fix: Removed pharma-led model. Two options remain: Public Funded and Partnership (PPP). In the PPP model, pharma co-funds development as one of several funders — participation is scoped and transparent, not driving the project. Pharma’s commercial interest is documented as incentive alignment context, not a standalone investment thesis.

13. Development cost assumptions refined

Original: Dev cost slider range €150k–650k with €315k base case.

Fix: Slider range lowered to €80k–650k. Base case reduced to €200k (software ~€80–100k + integration ~€35–60k + validation ~€40–70k). This explicitly excludes MDR Class IIa medical device certification (€80–200k additional, flagged as a risk in the tooltip). Operations base case increased to €140k (full-stack dev ~€55–70k + part-time PM ~€30–40k + infra + analysis).

NET EFFECT OF ALL ADJUSTMENTS

Adjustments 1–2 (multi-touch screening + population-level pools) increase the number of diagnoses.

Adjustments 4–6 and 8 (exclusive savings, adherence, QALY, discounting) decrease per-patient savings and inflate cost/QALY.

Adjustments 3, 9 (FP depletion, GP cost) have minor mixed effects.

The model is now more defensible: it finds more patients (corrected screening logic) but claims less savings per patient (adherence, no double-counting, discounting). The headline numbers may look different from the first draft, but they rest on ground you can defend to a health economist.

PE Associate Review — Additional Fixes

14. Acute care savings ramp for early-stage patients

Critique: PUHELU catches mostly GOLD A/B patients with low near-term hospitalisation risk. Applying the full €509/pt/yr from Year 1 overstates early savings.

Fix: Acute care savings now ramp from 20% of the full rate in Year 0 (mild patients still avoid some ER/urgent visits) to 100% by Year 4 (as some of the cohort would have progressed to severe without treatment). This reduces Year 1 savings significantly but leaves the long-term trajectory intact.

15. Consistent discounting of costs and savings

Critique: The 3.5% discount rate was applied only to savings, not costs. Costs in future years should also be discounted to present value for a proper NPV comparison.

Fix: All cash flows — both costs and savings — are now discounted at 3.5% annually. The P&L shows present values throughout. This is standard DCF methodology.

16. Terminal value added

Critique: The model stopped at Year 5. Diagnosed patients continue generating savings for decades, and the system continues running. The 5-year ROI significantly understated the lifetime value.

Fix: Terminal value calculated as a growing perpetuity: Year 5 net savings ÷ (discount rate − 1% growth rate), discounted to present. The 1% growth rate is conservative (below healthcare cost inflation). Terminal value is shown as a separate KPI, not mixed into the 5-year P&L.

17. Physician engagement: learning curve replaces arbitrary thresholds

Critique: The ±3-5%/year drift based on PPV thresholds (45% and 25%) was arbitrary. Real physician behaviour change follows a learning curve over 2–3 years, not discrete annual steps.

Fix: Engagement now follows a logarithmic learning curve. Doctors build confidence over time as they see confirmed diagnoses from flags. Engagement drifts from the initial slider value toward a PPV-adjusted ceiling (high PPV → up to 75%, low PPV → down to 25%), with diminishing returns over ~5 years. This matches clinical informatics adoption literature where practice patterns plateau after 2–3 years.

18. Visits per year: documented constant, not a slider

Critique: The PE review suggested making visits/year a slider since it’s the single biggest driver of screening volume.

Decision: Kept as a documented constant at 3.0. This is a verifiable fact from Eurostat (Finland: 4.4 total consultations/capita/year, 2023) adjusted down for Helsinki + smoking-exposed population characteristics. It should not be an assumption to play with — it should be measured from Apotti appointment data in the pilot.

19. QALY lowered from 0.5 to 0.3 per detected case

Previous: 0.5 QALY/detected case, described as “optimistic end of literature.”

Issue: Lambe et al. (Thorax 2019) reports 0.028 QALY/screened patient. Dividing by 8.8% COPD prevalence gives 0.32/COPD case. But not all gain is incremental — some cases would have been diagnosed through usual care. 0.5 overstated the incremental benefit.

Fix: Lowered to 0.3 QALY/detected case. Conservative relative to the 0.32 derivation, accounting for non-incremental diagnoses. Cost/QALY increases ~67% but remains well below Finnish thresholds.

20. National expansion built into model

Previous: Model only showed Helsinki/Apotti pilot (115,000 eligible patients, 1.7M population). Did not account for national rollout.

Issue: The whole pitch is “pilot in Helsinki, then scale nationally.” Showing only Helsinki understates the opportunity by ~3.3×. All 4 Finnish EHR systems support FHIR R4.

Fix: Population multiplier ramps over 5 years: Year 0–1 Helsinki/Apotti (1×), Year 2 +Lifecare (2.2×), Year 3+ full national (3.3×). Each expansion adds EHR integration costs (€120k Lifecare, €150k OMNI360+Esko). Operations scale sub-linearly (shared infrastructure). New undiagnosed and non-COPD pools are added when regions come online.

21. Generic pharma partner (was: AstraZeneca-specific)

Previous: Model explicitly named AstraZeneca throughout, framing the project around their product portfolio.

Fix: Replaced with generic “Pharma Partner.” The model now shows what any respiratory pharma company gains: real-world evidence, disease awareness, EU replication model, health system partnerships. The 12% weighted market share applies to any single partner with an ICS-focused portfolio. Compliant with EFPIA and Finnish pharmaceutical industry (Lääketeollisuus ry) codes.

22. Physician engagement simplified

Previous: Logarithmic learning curve with PPV-adjusted ceiling. Engagement drifted over time based on flag accuracy.

Fix: Reverted to flat rate from slider. The learning curve added complexity with marginal impact and no empirical basis for the specific curve parameters. The pilot’s primary purpose is to measure this rate, not predict it from a model. The slider IS the assumption.

23. All citations verified against primary sources

Issue: Earlier research agents fabricated several citations (Lokke 2012, Jordan 2014 Thorax, Castillo 2019, TLV Sweden). These papers either don’t exist or don’t contain the claimed data.

Fix: All tooltips now cite only verified sources: Lambe et al. (Thorax 2019, PMC6703126) for QALY. Anthonisen et al. (AJRCCM 2002) for progression rate. Herse et al. (npj PCRM 2015) and Viinanen et al. (Int J COPD 2019) for Finnish unit costs. Lisspers et al. (Int J COPD 2018, ARCTIC study) for early-dx savings. Restrepo et al. (Int J COPD 2008) for adherence. Eurostat 2023 for visit frequency.
